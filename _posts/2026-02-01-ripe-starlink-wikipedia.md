Two interesting blog posts on [RIPE Labs](https://labs.ripe.net/) explore the Internet Routing Registries. These databases contain information about the prefixes originated by various Autonomous Systems. The [first post](https://labs.ripe.net/author/stavros-konstantaras/the-irr-landscape-where-do-ases-keep-their-routes/) explained the operation of these registries. The [second post](https://labs.ripe.net/author/tobias-striffler/the-irr-landscape-data-quality-the-good-the-bad-and-the-outdated/) analyzes the quality of the data store in these registries. 

Ookla reveals interesting data about the [uplink capacity in 5G networks](https://www.ookla.com/articles/analyzing-the-uplink-in-the-age-of-ai).

Job Snijder published his yearly summary of the evolution of the RPKI on the [NANOG mailing list](https://lists.nanog.org/archives/list/nanog@lists.nanog.org/thread/QLG35AUUHHZVLF6D24LK5J6HCOQQWL6V/).

Nice [video](https://youtu.be/xYfiOnufBSk?si=zbmmO6kkh29GgH61) showing how passkeys work to authenticate users on web sites.

Luc de Brandere [posted on LinkedIn](https://www.linkedin.com/feed/update/urn:li:activity:7419311376336166912/) a really nice drawing by Alix Garin to celebrate the 25th birthday of Wikipedia which continues to operate outside today’s surveillance economy.

A [blog post](https://taranis.ie/datacenters-in-space-are-a-terrible-horrible-no-good-idea/) explains why putting datacenters in space is a really bad idea in case you thought about it. Another [blog post](http://www.physicsmatt.com/blog/2025/12/11/the-dumbest-thing-ive-seen-this-week) also explains why AI datacenters in space are a bad idea. On a related topic, Margo Anderson discusses in [IEEE Spectrum](https://spectrum.ieee.org/kessler-syndrome-crash-clock) the increased saturation of the low orbits that are used by constellations like Starlink.

There are more and more airplanes that provide Internet connectivity. Thomas Dreibholz provides [detailed measurements](https://www.nntb.no/2026/01/30/a-sleepless-night-over-the-atlantic-ocean-analysing-the-satellite-network-connectivity-during-a-trans-atlantic-flight/) of the performance of his connection on a transatlantic flight. 

The Internet Engineering Task Force (IETF) held its first meeting [forty years ago](https://www.ietf.org/blog/ietf-40/) in San Diego. There were 21 participants. Today more than 8000 people participate to the IETF.
